# Live-in-Labs — Rural Technology Research

**Duration:** October 2025 – December 2025  
**Location:** Nediamanickam Village, Tamil Nadu  
**Institution:** Amrita Vishwa Vidyapeetham  
**Role:** Research Intern — Rural Technology  
**Methodologies:** Human-Centered Design (HCD), Participatory Rural Appraisal (PRA)

## Overview

Live-in-Labs is Amrita Vishwa Vidyapeetham's flagship experiential learning program where students live in under-served rural communities, identify real problems through structured research methodologies, and co-design sustainable solutions with villagers. Ganesh was deployed to Nediamanickam village in Tamil Nadu, where his team focused on **water quality and energy access** challenges.

This experience became a defining chapter in Ganesh's identity — the moment where his high-tech digital skillset collided with ground-level rural reality, forging what he calls his "dual-world" engineering philosophy.

---

## Research Methodology

### Human-Centered Design (HCD)
- Conducted empathy-building interviews with village households to understand daily water usage patterns, health complaints linked to water quality, and existing coping mechanisms.
- Created journey maps of villagers' water sourcing workflows — from collection at borewells to storage and consumption.
- Iterated on solution concepts through participatory prototyping sessions where villagers provided direct feedback on proposed designs.

### Participatory Rural Appraisal (PRA)
- Facilitated community mapping exercises to document water sources, contamination points, and infrastructure gaps.
- Conducted seasonal calendars and livelihood analyses to understand when water stress peaks.
- Used wealth ranking and focus group discussions to ensure solutions were equitable across economic strata within the village.
- Engaged village elders and women's self-help groups as primary stakeholders — recognizing that women bear the operational burden of water management.

## The Water Purification System

### Problem Identification
Field testing and community interviews revealed that the village's primary water sources suffered from:
- Elevated levels of dissolved minerals and microbial contamination
- Inconsistent supply during dry seasons
- Lack of affordable, maintainable purification infrastructure

Commercially available RO systems were economically unviable for the community and required reliable electricity and maintenance expertise not locally available.

### Solution Design
The team designed a **gravity-fed, multi-stage water purification system** optimized for:
- **Zero electricity requirement** — critical for a community with inconsistent power supply
- **Locally sourceable filter media** — using sand, gravel, and activated charcoal layers that villagers could replace themselves
- **Low maintenance threshold** — designed so that the system could be maintained by community members with a simple pictorial guide
- **Scalability** — modular design allowing additional filtration stages as resources become available

### Design Principles Applied
- **Appropriate Technology:** Rejected high-tech solutions in favor of robust, repairable designs
- **Community Ownership:** Ensured the solution could be operated and maintained without external technical dependence
- **Cost Transparency:** All materials costed and documented so the community understood total ownership cost

## The Solar Panel Decision

One of the most formative moments of the experience was the team's analysis of whether to recommend solar panel installation for the village.

### Initial Hypothesis
Solar panels seemed like an obvious solution — the village had inconsistent grid electricity and abundant sunlight.

### Research Findings
After detailed cost-benefit analysis, the team concluded **against** solar panel recommendation:
- **Upfront cost** exceeded what the village panchayat could fund without external grants
- **Maintenance complexity** — inverter and battery replacements required technical expertise not available locally
- **Payback period** extended beyond practical planning horizons for the community
- **Government grid expansion** plans were already underway for the region, making solar a redundant investment within 2-3 years

### The Lesson
This decision taught Ganesh that **engineering morality means recommending what's right, not what's impressive.** The instinct to deploy cutting-edge technology had to be overridden by honest analysis of the community's actual constraints. It was the first time he experienced the tension between technologist ambition and ground-level responsibility.

## Bridging Tech with Rural Reality

The Live-in-Labs experience crystallized Ganesh's "Dual Identity" — the realization that he operates in two worlds:

1. **The Digital World:** Cyberpunk aesthetic, AI agents, offline-first architectures, systems-level thinking
2. **The Rural World:** Hand-drawn village maps, gravity-fed filters, seasonal water stress, pictorial maintenance guides

Rather than seeing these as contradictions, Ganesh understood them as **complementary capabilities.** The same systems thinking that designs a Riverpod state architecture can design a community water management workflow. The same offline-first philosophy that drives RiceAgent Pro's Drift ORM design was born from watching a village operate without reliable connectivity.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Technology is a tool, not a solution.** The best engineering sometimes means recommending the simplest, most maintainable option.
- **Empathy is a design requirement.** HCD and PRA aren't academic exercises — they're the difference between a solution that gets used and one that gets abandoned.
- **Sustainability means local ownership.** If the community can't maintain it without you, you haven't solved the problem.
- **The dual-world perspective is a competitive advantage.** Very few engineers can context-switch between AI agent architectures and rural water purification design. This range is Ganesh's unique value proposition.
